50 Sec

Film

In a fifty-second one-take courtroom film,Jurisdiction follows a tense legal momentwhere questions of authority, responsibility,and unresolved justice are compressed intoone continuous shot

Watch Film

Personal Statement

Jurisdiction was my first assignment for Introduction to Filmmaking, and it was built around thechallenge of making a complete idea in only fifty seconds. The film was a courtroom situation, but Iwanted it to feel bigger than just a normal court scene. I was interested in the feeling of being judged,questioned, and placed under pressure by a system that is supposed to have authority. The titleJurisdiction mattered to me because it connects to the idea of who has the right to decide, and whathappens when that right is unclear.The assignment was also difficult because it had to be filmed as one continuous take with no cuts. Thismade me think more carefully about timing, movement, and blocking. Since I shot it handheld on aSamsung Galaxy S25 Ultra, I had to practice how to move smoothly and keep the camera steadywithout professional equipment. I also had to coordinate the actors so that the dialogue, entrances,and positions all worked inside the fifty-second limit. It made the project feel almost like a small liveperformance, because if one thing went wrong, the whole take had to be repeated.What I learned from this project is that short films can be very demanding because there is no space towaste. Every movement, pause, and sound has to help the scene. I also learned that research cansupport even a short project, because looking into courtroom procedures, legal authority, and visualreferences helped me understand the mood I wanted. Jurisdiction helped me start thinking aboutfilmmaking not only as recording something, but as planning tension, atmosphere, and meaning insidea very limited frame.

5 Shots

Sequence

In a simple five-shot sequence, a personsitting at his desk scrolls on his phone whilehis laptop stays open, showing a quietmoment of distraction and digital routine

Watch Film

Personal Statement

For the five-shots assignment, I wanted to work with a very simple everyday situation: a guy sitting athis desk, scrolling, while his laptop is open in front of him. Since the assignment still needs to be fullycompleted, I am thinking of it as a small visual study of routine, distraction, and how people can bephysically present at work but mentally somewhere else. The story is not based on a big event, but on afamiliar moment that almost everyone understands.The main challenge of this assignment is to make the scene clear using only five shots. I need to chooseeach shot carefully so that the viewer understands the space, the character, and the mood withoutneeding too much explanation. I can use one shot to establish the desk, another to show the phone orscrolling, another to show the open laptop, and then closer shots to show the feeling of being stuckbetween work and distraction. This makes me think more about visual storytelling and how a sequencecan be built from small details.What I want to learn from this project is how shot selection changes the meaning of a scene. A personscrolling at a desk could look boring if it is filmed without intention, but it can also say something aboutfocus, procrastination, tiredness, or being overwhelmed by screens. For me, this assignment is achance to practice making an ordinary action feel more cinematic through framing, order, and rhythm

Watch Film

Personal Statement

For my sense of place project, I chose to capture Ifrane through the people and moments that give thecity life. I filmed children playing, people spending time outside, others working, and different scenesthat felt natural and alive. I did not want the project to only show buildings or landscapes. I wanted it toshow the rhythm of the place through the people who move inside it.This assignment made me pay more attention to details that I might normally pass by without thinking.When I was filming, I started noticing gestures, sounds, faces, and small actions that say a lot about aplace. Ifrane can look calm and quiet, but when I looked closer, there were many vivid momentshappening at the same time. That contrast became important to me.Through this project, I learned that filming a place is also about patience and observation. I had to waitfor moments instead of controlling everything. It helped me understand documentary-style shootingmore, because sometimes the strongest images are the ones that happen naturally. This project mademe appreciate how everyday life can become cinematic when I actually take the time to look at it.

Scene Remake

Video

In this remake of the “I love The Smiths”scene from 500 Days of Summer, twocharacters connect through music in a smallmoment that feels simple but emotionallyimportant

Watch Film

Personal Statement

For the scene remake assignment, I chose the “I love The Smiths” scene from 500 Days of Summer. Iliked this scene because it is not dramatic in a big way, but it still feels memorable. The moment issmall: two people connect because of music, and suddenly there is a shift between them. I found thatinteresting because it shows how cinema can make a very normal interaction feel meaningful.Remaking the scene was challenging because I had to respect the original while also accepting that myversion would not be exactly the same. I had to think about the actors, the location, the timing, andhow to make the scene feel natural. The original scene depends a lot on subtle reactions, soperformance was very important. If the reactions felt too forced, the whole scene would lose its charm.This assignment taught me that recreating a scene is a good way to understand how it works. When Iwatched it as a viewer, it seemed simple, but when I had to remake it, I realized how much depends onpacing, framing, and the actors’ expressions. It also made me think about music as a way of buildingconnection between characters, which is something I became more interested in throughout the class.

One More Minute

Video

In a personal two-character scene inspiredby “One More Hour” by Tame Impala, twopeople face the pain of leaving and theemotional weight of accepting change.

Watch Film

Personal Statement

This two-character scene was one of the most personal projects I worked on. The story is aboutsomeone leaving and having to deal with that moment emotionally. It was inspired by the song “OneMore Hour” by Tame Impala, especially the feeling of time running out and wanting to stay in a momenteven when you know it is ending. I connected with that feeling, and I wanted the scene to carry thatsame emotional weight.What made this project important to me was that it came from a real feeling, not just an assignmentidea. I wanted the characters to speak and act in a way that felt close to real life. Sometimes leaving isnot expressed through a big argument or dramatic speech. Sometimes it is quiet, uncomfortable, andfull of things that are difficult to say. I tried to bring that kind of honesty into the scene.During production, I learned how hard it can be to direct or shape something that is personal. When thesubject is close to you, every line and every pause feels important. At the same time, it helped me caremore about the details. I learned that music can inspire not only the mood of a film, but also theemotional rhythm of a scene. This project helped me understand how personal experiences canbecome a starting point for storytelling.

DocPortrait

Video

This documentary portrait follows IdirMoukhlis, an AUI alumnus and currentemployee, by highlighting his work, hisconnection to the university, and hisprofessional journey.

Watch Film

Personal Statement

For my documentary portrait, I chose to focus on Idir Moukhlis, who is an AUI alumnus and now worksat the university. I found his story interesting because he is connected to AUI in more than one way. Hewas once a student, and now he is part of the university as an employee. That gave the portrait a senseof continuity, because his relationship with the place did not end after graduation.This project was different from the fictional assignments because I had to work with a real person and areal story. I wanted to represent him honestly and respectfully. Instead of inventing emotions orsituations, I had to listen, observe, and choose what parts of his work and experience would bestcommunicate who he is. That made me more aware of the responsibility that comes with documentaryfilmmaking.Through this assignment, I learned more about interviewing and building a portrait through details. Adocumentary is not only about recording information. It is also about choosing the right moments,creating a comfortable space for the subject, and finding a structure that allows the audience tounderstand the person. This project helped me see how film can preserve someone’s experience andgive attention to people we may see every day without really knowing their story.

Personal Statement

The video clip assignment was probably one of the hardest projects for me because I produced thesong myself. It took a lot out of me, and honestly it was not an easy process at all. I learnedthat producing something original can be frustrating, but it also makes the final work feel moreconnected to me. It pushed me to combine different parts of my creativity, especially sound and image.Looking back, I think the difficulty of the process is part of what made the project meaningful.

Parti Pris

Video

This four-minute historical documentaryexplores the political legacy of AbderrahimBouabid through archival material,interviews, and a directorial point of viewfocused on his influence on Moroccanpolitics.

Watch Film

Personal Statement

My final class project, which was also my parti pris project, was a four-minute historical documentaryabout Abderrahim Bouabid. The project focused on his political legacy in Morocco, his role inimportant political movements, and the historical ideas connected to his work, including therelationship between his political activity and the late Hassan 2. Since the project involved theAbderrahim Bouabid Foundation and Ali Bouabid, I felt that I was not only making a class assignment,but also working with a subject that had real historical weight.Because this was a parti pris project, I had to think more clearly about my own directorial stance. I wasnot just presenting information in a neutral way. I wanted the documentary to emphasize Bouabid’sinfluence and the importance of his place in Moroccan political history. The visual style came mainlyfrom archival research, including historical footage, political pins, and other materials that helpedconnect the viewer to the period and to the political atmosphere surrounding him. This made theproject different from my earlier work because I had to build meaning from existing historical materialand combine it with interviews in a way that still felt alive.The editing process was one of the most difficult parts of this project. Since the film had to fit into fourminutes, I had to make choices about what to keep, what to cut, and how to structure the narrative so itdid not feel rushed. A lot of time went into organizing the interview footage and shaping thedocumentary into a clear arc. I also learned that credits matter, because the project involvedcollaboration and brainstorming, especially with my supervisor and friend. This final project helped meunderstand how documentary filmmaking can be both historical and personal, because even when thesubject is from the past, the way I choose to frame it says something about my own perspective as afilmmaker.
