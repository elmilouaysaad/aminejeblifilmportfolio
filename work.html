50 Sec

In a fifty-second one-take courtroom film, Jurisdiction follows a tense legal moment where questions of authority, responsibility, and unresolved justice are compressed into one continuous shot.

Personal Statement

Jurisdiction was my first assignment for Introduction to Filmmaking, and it was built around the challenge of making a complete idea in only fifty seconds. The film was a courtroom situation, but I wanted it to feel bigger than just a normal court scene. I was interested in the feeling of being judged, questioned, and placed under pressure by a system that is supposed to have authority. The title Jurisdiction mattered to me because it connects to the idea of who has the right to decide, and what happens when that right is unclear.

The assignment was also difficult because it had to be filmed as one continuous take with no cuts. This made me think more carefully about timing, movement, and blocking. Since I shot it handheld on a Samsung Galaxy S25 Ultra, I had to practice how to move smoothly and keep the camera steady without professional equipment. I also had to coordinate the actors so that the dialogue, entrances, and positions all worked inside the fifty-second limit. It made the project feel almost like a small live performance, because if one thing went wrong, the whole take had to be restarted.

What I learned from this project is that short films can be very demanding because there is no space to waste. Every movement, pause, and sound has to help the scene. I also learned that research can support even a short project, because looking into courtroom procedures, legal authority, and visual references helped me understand the mood I wanted. Jurisdiction helped me start thinking about filmmaking not only as recording something, but as planning tension, atmosphere, and meaning inside a very limited frame.

5 Shots

In a simple five-shot sequence, a person sitting at his desk scrolls on his phone while his laptop stays open, showing a quiet moment of distraction and digital routine.

Personal Statement

For the five-shots assignment, I wanted to work with a very simple everyday situation: a guy sitting at his desk, scrolling, while his laptop is open in front of him. Since the assignment still needs to be fully completed, I am thinking of it as a small visual study of routine, distraction, and how people can be physically present at work but mentally somewhere else. The story is not based on a big event, but on a familiar moment that almost everyone recognizes.

The main challenge of this assignment is to make the scene clear using only five shots. I need to choose each shot carefully so that the viewer understands the space, the character, and the mood without needing too much explanation. I can use one shot to establish the desk, another to show the phone or scrolling, another to show the open laptop, and then closer shots to show the feeling of being stuck between work and distraction. This makes me think more about visual storytelling and how a sequence can be built from small decisions.

What I want to learn from this project is how shot selection changes the meaning of a scene. A person scrolling at a desk could look boring if it is filmed without intention, but it can also say something about focus, procrastination, tiredness, or being overwhelmed by screens. For me, this assignment is a chance to practice making an ordinary action feel more cinematic through framing, order, and rhythm.

Sense of Place

This sense of place project captures everyday life in Ifrane through vivid moments of children playing, people working, and ordinary movement around the city.

Personal Statement

For my sense of place project, I chose to capture Ifrane through the people and moments that give the city life. I filmed children playing, people spending time outside, others working, and different scenes that felt natural and alive. I did not want the project to only show buildings or landscapes. I wanted it to show the rhythm of the place through the people who move inside it.

This assignment made me pay more attention to details that I might normally pass by without thinking. When I was filming, I started noticing gestures, sounds, faces, and small actions that say a lot about a place. Ifrane can look calm and quiet, but when I looked closer, there were many vivid moments happening at the same time. That contrast became important to me.

Through this project, I learned that filming a place is also about patience and observation. I had to wait for moments instead of controlling everything. It helped me understand documentary-style shooting more, because sometimes the strongest images are the ones that happen naturally. This project made me appreciate how everyday life can become cinematic when I actually take the time to look at it.

Scene Remake

In this remake of the "I love The Smiths" scene from 500 Days of Summer, two characters connect through music in a small moment that feels simple but emotionally important.

Personal Statement

For the scene remake assignment, I chose the "I love The Smiths" scene from 500 Days of Summer. I liked this scene because it is not dramatic in a big way, but it still feels memorable. The moment is small: two people connect because of music, and suddenly there is a shift between them. I found that interesting because it shows how cinema can make a very normal interaction feel meaningful.

Remaking the scene was challenging because I had to respect the original while also accepting that my version would not be exactly the same. I had to think about the actors, the location, the timing, and how to make the scene feel natural. The original scene depends a lot on subtle reactions, so performance was very important. If the reactions felt too forced, the whole scene would lose its quality.

This assignment taught me that recreating a scene is a good way to understand how it works. When I watched it as a viewer, it seemed simple, but when I had to remake it, I realized how much depends on pacing, framing, and the actors' expressions. It also made me think about music as a way of building connection between characters, which is something I became more interested in throughout the course.

One More Minute

In a personal two-character scene inspired by "One More Hour" by Tame Impala, two people face the pain of leaving and the emotional weight of accepting change.

Personal Statement

This two-character scene was one of the most personal projects I worked on. The story is about someone leaving and having to deal with that moment emotionally. It was inspired by the song "One More Hour" by Tame Impala, especially the feeling of time running out and wanting to stay in a moment even when you know it is ending. I connected with that feeling, and I wanted the scene to carry that same emotional weight.

What made this project important to me was that it came from a real feeling, not just an assignment idea. I wanted the characters to speak and act in a way that felt close to real life. Sometimes leaving is not expressed through a big argument or dramatic speech. Sometimes it is quiet, uncomfortable, and full of things that are difficult to say. I tried to bring that kind of honesty into the scene.

During production, I learned how hard it can be to direct or shape something that is personal. When the subject is close to you, every line and every pause feels important. At the same time, it helped me care more about the details. I learned that music can inspire not only the mood of a film, but also the emotional rhythm of a scene. This project helped me understand how personal experiences can become a starting point for storytelling.

DocPortrait

This documentary portrait follows Idir Moukhlis, an AUI alumnus and current employee, by highlighting his work, his connection to the university, and his professional journey.

Personal Statement

For my documentary portrait, I chose to focus on Idir Moukhlis, who is an AUI alumnus and now works at the university. I found his story interesting because he is connected to AUI in more than one way. He was once a student, and now he is part of the university as an employee. That gave the portrait a sense of continuity, because his relationship with the place did not end after graduation.

This project was different from the fictional assignments because I had to work with a real person and a real story. I wanted to represent him honestly and respectfully. Instead of inventing emotions or situations, I had to listen, observe, and choose what parts of his work and experience would best communicate who he is. That made me more aware of the responsibility that comes with documentary filmmaking.

Through this assignment, I learned more about interviewing and building a portrait through details. A documentary is not only about recording information. It is also about choosing the right moments, creating a comfortable space for the subject, and finding a structure that allows the audience to understand the person. This project helped me see how film can preserve someone's experience and give attention to people we may see every day without really knowing their story.

NightSky

For this video clip assignment, I created both the visuals and the music, turning the difficulty of producing an original song into the main creative challenge of the project.

Personal Statement

The video clip assignment was probably one of the hardest projects for me because I produced the song myself. It took a lot out of me, and honestly it was not an easy process at all. Making the music already felt like a full project on its own, and then I also had to think about how to create visuals that matched it. This made the assignment feel intense, but also very personal because the sound was mine from the beginning.

Since I produced the song, I felt more responsible for the final result. I was not just choosing a track and filming something over it. I had to think about the mood, rhythm, and structure of the song while also imagining what kind of images could support it. This made me realize how closely music and film can work together. A small change in the song can change the feeling of a shot, and a visual choice can change how the music is heard.

Even though it took me hell, this project taught me a lot about patience and creative pressure. I learned that producing something original can be frustrating, but it also makes the final work feel more connected to me. It pushed me to combine different parts of my creativity, especially sound and image. Looking back, I think the difficulty of the process is part of what made the project meaningful.

Parti Pris

This four-minute historical documentary explores the political legacy of Abderrahim Bouabid through archival material, interviews, and a directorial point of view focused on his influence on Moroccan politics.

Personal Statement

My final class project, which was also my parti pris project, was a four-minute historical documentary about Abderrahim Bouabid. The project focused on his political legacy in Morocco, his role in important political movements, and the historical ideas connected to his work, including the relationship between his political activity and the late Hassan 2. Since the project involved the Abderrahim Bouabid Foundation and Ali Bouabid, I felt that I was not only making a class assignment, but also working with a subject that had real historical weight.

Because this was a parti pris project, I had to think more clearly about my own directorial stance. I was not just presenting information in a neutral way. I wanted the documentary to emphasize Bouabid's influence and the importance of his place in Moroccan political history. The visual style came mainly from archival research, including historical footage, political pins, and other materials that helped connect the viewer to the period and to the political atmosphere surrounding him. This made the project different from my earlier work because I had to build meaning from existing historical material and combine it with interviews in a way that still felt coherent.

The editing process was one of the most difficult parts of this project. Since the film had to fit into four minutes, I had to make choices about what to keep, what to cut, and how to structure the narrative so it did not feel rushed. A lot of time went into organizing the interview footage and shaping the documentary into a clear arc. I also learned that credits matter, because the project involved collaboration and brainstorming, especially with my supervisor and friend. This final project helped me understand how documentary filmmaking can be both historical and personal, because even when the subject is from the past, the way I choose to frame it says something about my own perspective as a filmmaker.
